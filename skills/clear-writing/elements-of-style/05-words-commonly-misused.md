# Words and Expressions Commonly Misused

Word choice guide from Strunk's Elements of Style. Some forms here are poor English; others are acceptable but disfavored; some are good but overused.

## A

**affect / effect**
- Affect = influence (verb): "The weather affects my mood."
- Effect = result (noun): "The effect was immediate."
- Effect = bring about (verb): "She effected a change in policy."

**all right**
- Two words. Not "alright."
- In familiar speech: "Agreed" or "Go ahead"

**as good or better than**
- Awkward. Rearrange.
- Wrong: "My opinion is as good or better than his."
- Right: "My opinion is as good as his, or better."

**as to whether**
- Just use "whether."

## B

**but**
- Unnecessary after *doubt* and *help*: "I don't doubt that" (not "but that")
- Avoid consecutive *but* conjunctions

## C

**can / may**
- Can = able
- May = permitted

**case**
- Usually unnecessary.
- Wrong: "In many cases, the rooms were poorly ventilated."
- Right: "Many rooms were poorly ventilated."

**certainly**
- Avoid as vague intensifier. If you mean it, leave it; if not, delete it.

**character**
- Often redundant.
- Wrong: "Acts of a hostile character"
- Right: "Hostile acts"

**claim**
- With object-noun: means "lay claim to"
- Don't substitute for *declare*, *maintain*, or *charge*

**clever**
- Overused. Restrict to ingenuity in small matters.

**compare to / compare with**
- Compare TO: resemblance between different things. "Life compared to a pilgrimage."
- Compare WITH: differences between similar things. "Congress compared with Parliament."

**consider**
- Not followed by *as* when meaning "believe to be."
- Wrong: "I consider him as competent."
- Right: "I consider him competent."

## D

**data**
- Plural form (like phenomena, strata).
- "The data are" not "the data is."

**dependable**
- Needless substitute for *reliable* or *trustworthy*.

**different from**
- Not "different than."
- Use *different from*, *other than*, or *unlike*.

**divided into / composed of**
- Plays are divided INTO acts.
- Poems are composed OF stanzas.

**due to**
- Only as predicate or modifier to specific noun.
- Wrong: "He lost the election due to poor campaigning."
- Right: "His loss was due to poor campaigning."
- Better: "He lost because of poor campaigning."

## E

**etc.**
- Equivalent to "and the rest."
- Don't use if reader would doubt what the rest is.
- Never use after *such as* or *for example*.

## F

**fact**
- Use only for matters of direct verification.
- Napoleon's escape is a fact.
- Napoleon's greatness is a judgment, not a fact.

**factor**
- Hackneyed. Replace with direct expression.
- Wrong: "Training was a factor in his winning."
- Right: "He won by training harder."

**feature**
- Another hackneyed word usually adding nothing.
- Avoid as verb in advertising sense.

**fewer / less**
- Fewer = number: "Fewer errors"
- Less = quantity: "Less time"
- Never "less errors."

**fix**
- Colloquial in America. In writing, restrict to literary senses like *fasten*.

## G-H

**get**
- Don't use colloquial "have got" for *have* in writing.

**he is a man who**
- Redundant.
- Wrong: "He is a man who is very ambitious."
- Right: "He is very ambitious."

**however**
- In meaning "nevertheless," don't place first in sentence.
- Wrong: "However, we succeeded."
- Right: "At last, however, we succeeded."
- When first: means "in whatever way"

## I-K

**interesting**
- Avoid as perfunctory introduction.
- Don't tell reader something is interesting. Make it interesting.

**kind of / sort of**
- Don't substitute for *rather* or *something like* except in familiar style.
- Restrict to literal sense: "Amber is a kind of fossil resin."

## L

**less**
- See **fewer / less**.

**like / as**
- Like governs nouns and pronouns.
- As for phrases and clauses.
- Wrong: "We spent the evening like in the old days."
- Right: "We spent the evening as in the old days."

**literally**
- Often incorrectly used to support exaggeration.
- Wrong: "A literal flood of abuse."
- Right: "A flood of abuse." (Metaphor needs no support.)

**lose out**
- Less emphatic than *lose*. Omit *out*.

## M-N

**most**
- Don't use for *almost*.
- Wrong: "Most everybody agreed."
- Right: "Almost everybody agreed."

**nature**
- Often redundant like *character*.
- Vague without specifics: "a lover of nature" could mean anything.

## O

**oftentimes / ofttimes**
- Archaic. Use *often*.

**one of the most**
- Avoid as essay opener. Threadbare.
- Use plural verb: "One of the ablest men that have" not "has."

## P

**people / the public**
- People = political term
- Public = commercial/artistic term
- People provide political support; public provides artistic appreciation.

**phase**
- Means development stage.
- Don't use for *aspect* or *topic*.

**possess**
- Don't substitute for *have* or *own*.
- Wrong: "He possessed great courage."
- Right: "He had great courage."

## R-S

**respective / respectively**
- Usually omit with advantage.
- Wrong: "Works listed under their respective authors."
- Right: "Works listed under the authors' names."

**shall / will**
- Future tense: *shall* for first person, *will* for second/third.
- "I shall" = simple future
- "I will" = determination

**so**
- Avoid as intensifier: not "so good" or "so warm."

**split infinitive**
- Avoid interposing adverb between *to* and infinitive.
- Wrong: "to diligently inquire"
- Right: "to inquire diligently"

**state**
- Don't substitute for *say* or *remark*.
- Restrict to "express fully or clearly."

**student body**
- Awkward. Use *students*.

**system**
- Frequently used without need.
- Wrong: "commission system of government"
- Right: "government by commission"

## T

**that / which**
- That = restrictive (essential to meaning): "The book that you gave me."
- Which = non-restrictive (additional info): "The book, which was red."

**they**
- Don't use plural pronoun with distributive antecedents requiring singular.
- Wrong: "Everyone brought their lunch."
- Acceptable workaround: "All brought their lunches."

## V

**very**
- Use sparingly. Employ inherently strong words for emphasis.

**viewpoint**
- Write *point of view*.
- Don't misuse for *view* or *opinion*.

## W

**while**
- Restrict to "during the time that."
- Don't substitute for *and*, *but*, or *although*.
- Wrong: "I like tea, while he likes coffee."
- Right: "I like tea, but he likes coffee."

**whom**
- Use *who* when it's subject of following verb.
- "His brother, who he said would send the money." (who = subject of "would send")

**worth while**
- Overworked vague term.
- Strictly applicable to actions, never before nouns.
- Wrong: "Books are not worth while."
- Right: "Not worth reading."

**would**
- First person conditional requires *should*.
- Right: "I should not have succeeded without help."
- Wrong: "I would not have succeeded without help."
